Basic Information

Simplified vs. Traditional

Chinese is written in two main systems today. Simplified Chinese was introduced in mainland China in the 1950s to improve literacy, reducing the number of strokes in many characters. Traditional Chinese preserves the older, more complex forms of the characters and is used in many overseas Chinese communities. This website uses Simplified Chinese characters.

What are Radicals?

In Chinese, most characters are not standalone symbols, they are built from smaller components and have one component that is considered a radical. A radical is a recurring visual element that often hints at a character's meaning or category.

Word Meaning vs. Radical Meaning

Each character on this site has two meanings listed. The character meaning is the direct translation of the word itself. The radical meaning is broader, it describes the conceptual category the character belongs to when used as a component inside other characters.

Oracle, Bronze and Seal Scripts

Chinese characters have evolved over thousands of years through several major script forms. The earliest, Oracle Script, was carved onto bones and shells and often looked like simple drawings. This developed into Bronze Script, which appeared on ritual vessels and became more stylized. Later, Seal Script standardized character shapes with smooth, balanced lines. While additional scripts like Clerical and Regular Script came afterward, these three stages are the most important for understanding how characters developed from pictorial symbols into the structured forms used today.
